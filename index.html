1. Behind the Scenes

The development of Shadow Platformer began as a personal challenge to recreate the nostalgic experience of classic Mario-style games using Unreal Engine. My aim was to create a basic but fully playable platformer game with features like jumping, enemy interaction, and coin collection ‚Äî while improving my skills in visual scripting and level design.

I used Unreal Engine 5, relying mainly on Blueprints for logic and controls. I began by designing the player movement system: horizontal movement, gravity-based jumping, and collision with platforms. After that, I added interactive elements like coins, power-ups, and simple enemies that patrol specific areas.

Over the course of three weeks, I invested roughly 20‚Äì25 hours into learning, building, and testing. I followed tutorials, experimented independently, and referred to the Unreal community for debugging help. All assets were sourced from free or royalty-free platforms, with a few minor sprite modifications to personalize the look.

The feedback I received from classmates and lecturers was encouraging. They highlighted the polish of the movement system and the game‚Äôs responsiveness. Their suggestions, such as adding background music or fine-tuning enemy timing, helped me refine the final build.

2. Outcome

The final result was a fully playable prototype of a 2D side-scrolling platformer titled Shadow Platformer. Key features include:

  • Character Control: Smooth left/right movement, jump mechanics, and ground collision.
  • Level Design: A complete level with platforms, gaps, spikes, coins, and a finish goal.
  • Enemies: Simple patrol logic using Blueprints; enemies damage the player on contact.
  • UI: On-screen coin counter, health bar, and start/restart menus.
  • Sound: Background music and sound effects for jumping, collecting, and enemy contact.
  • Exportable Build: Game can be packaged and played as a standalone .exe file for Windows.

The game successfully demonstrates the core experience I set out to build ‚Äî fast-paced platforming with clear feedback and consistent logic. I also created promotional assets, including gameplay clips and screenshots, which I plan to use on my portfolio and social platforms.

3. Reflection

Working on Shadow Platformer deepened my understanding of Unreal Engine and game design in general. Even though it appears simple, building a platformer required attention to timing, hitboxes, animation syncing, and state management.

What worked well: The use of Blueprints allowed me to iterate quickly and visualize logic flows. My movement system felt responsive, and collision behavior was solid. I was also pleased with the simple enemy AI and how it added life to the level.

What didn‚Äôt work initially: Early in development, jump physics felt floaty and inconsistent. I also struggled with layering the background and tiles correctly, which caused visual glitches. Enemy logic also failed at times, with pathing bugs and overlap events triggering at the wrong times.

One of the main bugs I encountered was the player character falling off the map. This usually happened due to missing collisions or gaps between platforms.

I attempted to fix it by:

  • Adding invisible boundary walls or kill zones to respawn the player.
  • Improving collision detection on platforms.
  • Adjusting gravity and jump strength in the Blueprint settings.

This helped me better understand how Unreal Engine handles physics and level design, and improved my problem-solving skills.

How I overcame challenges: I revisited the Blueprint logic and broke it into smaller, testable parts. I also learned about collision channels and began using debug visuals to identify problem areas. Through iteration, community feedback, and self-testing, I improved performance and game flow.

This project taught me the importance of modular design, clear logic, and playtesting. It also boosted my confidence in using Unreal Engine for 2D game development. In the future, I aim to explore more advanced systems like health regeneration, multiple levels, and even simple boss fights ‚Äî potentially expanding the project or starting a new one in Unity or Godot for comparison.
